Tribute to Mahatma Gandhi

About Mahatma Gandhi

Mahatma Gandhi, known as the Father of the Nation in India, was a leader of the Indian independence movement against British rule. He is famous for his nonviolent philosophy of passive resistance and civil disobedience.

Early Life and Education

Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi was born on October 2, 1869, in Porbandar, India. He studied law in London and later practiced law in South Africa, where he experienced racial discrimination and began his journey as a social activist.

The Salt March

In 1930, Gandhi led the famous Salt March, a nonviolent protest against the British salt monopoly. The march lasted 24 days and covered 240 miles, from Sabarmati Ashram in Ahmedabad to the Arabian Sea. This event sparked a nationwide civil disobedience movement and brought international attention to the Indian independence struggle.

Nonviolent Resistance

Gandhi's philosophy of nonviolent resistance, known as Satyagraha, was a key factor in India's independence movement. He believed in using peaceful means, such as civil disobedience and non-cooperation, to achieve political and social goals. His methods inspired civil rights movements around the world, including the work of Martin Luther King Jr. in the United States.

Legacy

Mahatma Gandhi's life and teachings continue to inspire people worldwide. He is remembered for his unwavering commitment to truth, nonviolence, and social justice. Gandhi's birthday, October 2, is celebrated as Gandhi Jayanti, a national holiday in India, and as the International Day of Nonviolence by the United Nations.
